Why the timer is visible

A countdown converts a vague intention into a deadline you can see. That's timeboxing: instead of working on a task until it's done, you commit a fixed amount of time to it and let the boundary do the deciding. The constraint is the point — it forces a scope decision up front and blocks the drift into "just a bit longer."

Why blocks end before you're tired

Sustained attention degrades on its own. Performance on a single continuous task declines measurably over time — the vigilance decrement. Ariga and Lleras argued in 2011 that this isn't a fuel tank running dry but goal habituation: your brain stops registering a goal that never changes, the same way it tunes out a constant sound. Their fix was brief interruptions. Participants given two short diversions during a fifty-minute task held their accuracy flat while the control group's fell away.

Ariga & Lleras, Cognition 118(3), 2011

So the break isn't a reward for finishing. It's the mechanism. It deactivates the goal so it can be reactivated fresh.

Why rest scales with the block

A fixed five minutes is Pomodoro's convention, not a law. A 2022 meta-analysis of 22 micro-break studies found short breaks reliably reduced fatigue and raised vigor, but performance gains showed up mainly on less demanding tasks — and the longer the break, the bigger the boost. The authors' own conclusion was that recovering from heavy cognitive work likely needs more than ten minutes.

Albulescu et al., PLOS ONE 17(8), 2022

Hence proportional rest. A 10-minute block earns a token pause; a 50-minute block earns something that can actually restore you. The long rest after four blocks maps onto the same logic at a larger scale — your body cycles through roughly 90-minute activity troughs, and four short blocks plus their breaks lands near one.

Why it counts your overtime

You are systematically bad at estimating how long your own work takes — the planning fallacy, and it doesn't improve just from doing more work. It improves from feedback. Every finished block here records what you predicted against what you actually spent, which is the raw material for recalibration. Watch the accuracy figure in the log; if it sits at 140%, your instinct is to plan two hours of work into ninety minutes.

The overrun counter also makes stopping a deliberate act. An interrupted task stays noisier in memory than a finished one — the Zeigarnik effect — which is why leaving a block mid-sentence often makes the next one easier to start.

Where urgency stops helping

Arousal improves performance up to a point and then wrecks it. Amabile's diary study of 177 people found that time pressure, contrary to the folklore, generally reduced creative thinking. The exception mattered: high-pressure days still produced creative work when people felt they were on a mission — focused, protected from interruption, clear on why the deadline existed. When they felt like they were on a treadmill, being yanked between tasks, creativity collapsed.

Amabile, Hadley & Kramer, Harvard Business Review, Aug 2002

Practical reading: use tight blocks for defined, executional work — the inbox, the edit pass, the thing you've been avoiding. For genuinely open-ended thinking, set a long block and ignore the countdown, or don't arm it at all. The device is a tool, not a supervisor.

Operating notes

Name every block. A specific commitment — "outline section 3," not "writing" — is an implementation intention, and those survive contact with distraction far better than vague goals.

Take the rest away from the screen. Breaks restore attention when they're a real shift; scrolling keeps the same machinery running.

One minute is a legitimate block. If a task feels immovable, arm the smallest block you can't refuse. Starting is the expensive part.

The bomb is decoration. Nothing here is a real deadline unless you decide it is, and running a punishing schedule for weeks costs more than it earns.
